Destination Choice among Elite Biomedical Scientists

Working Paper

Pre-move Publication-Location Exposure and Destination Choice among Elite Biomedical Scientists

Research Question: Are subsequent mobility destinations more likely to be cities that were already visible in scientists’ origin-anchored pre-move publication-location environments?

Data: Mobility event × candidate city data for observed intercity moves by elite biomedical scientists.

Methods: Event-specific candidate destination sets, origin-anchored pre-move publication-location exposure, conditional logit, pre-move destination opportunity controls, geographic distance, timing robustness, and city-identity sensitivity checks.

Main Finding: Among observed mobility events, elite biomedical scientists are more likely to select cities that were already visible in their origin-anchored pre-move publication-location environments. The model does not show that exposure produces a move, and it treats publication-location exposure as destination visibility rather than interpersonal collaboration.

Global Mobility and Collaboration Networks of Elite Biomedical Scientists

Ongoing Research Program

Global Mobility and Collaboration Networks of Elite Biomedical Scientists

Research Question: How do elite biomedical scientists’ career moves, collaboration records, and city-level opportunity structures form spatial networks of scientific work?

Data: A 1970-2023 longitudinal database of 500 elite biomedical scientists, integrating career histories, institutional appointments, publication locations, collaboration records, mobility events, and city attributes.

Methods: Scientist-institution-city linkage, directed weighted mobility networks, centrality, core-periphery structure, K-core, WERGM/ERGM specifications, ego-network measures, and Python/R workflows for temporal auditing and documentation.

Contribution: This program provides the data and measurement foundation for the accepted article, the master’s thesis, and newer destination-choice work while keeping mobility events, publication locations, and collaboration records analytically separate.

Intercity Mobility Networks and Career Stages

Accepted Article and Thesis-Related Research

Spatial Structure of Top Biomedical Scientists’ Mobility

Research Question: What spatial structure characterizes global biomedical scientists’ intercity mobility, and how do associated factors differ across career stages?

Data: Career-history and intercity mobility records for top biomedical scientists, with thesis work extending to whole-network and ego-network perspectives.

Methods: Social network analysis, GIS spatial analysis, Heckman two-stage modeling for mobility-tie formation and intensity, and ego-network brokerage/diversity measures.

Contribution: The accepted article emphasizes urban network structure and mobility determinants. The master’s thesis extends the analysis by linking the whole intercity network with selected scientist-level ego networks.

Scopus Research Data Toolkit

Research Software · Python · v0.2.0

Scopus Research Data Toolkit

Research Question: How can Scopus-oriented bibliographic workflows be made reproducible and public-safe when nonpublic records and API responses cannot be released?

Data: Fully synthetic Scopus-style and OpenAlex-style fixtures, deterministic reference outputs, and offline mock transport.

Methods: Author-publication linkage, author-affiliation parsing, city-year publication controls, cognitive-proximity measures, source comparison, schema validation, manifests, and automated tests.

Contribution: The toolkit documents reusable measurement boundaries for publication and affiliation data. It is MIT licensed, includes 39 test functions in the current test suite, and defers live Scopus adapter behavior in v0.2.0. View repository

Selected Earlier Research

Earlier Research

Urban Resilience Measurement from Daily Nighttime Lights

Using VIIRS-DNB daily nighttime-light data for 48 cities in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei, Yangtze River Delta, and Pearl River Delta urban agglomerations, this project constructed high-frequency radiance time series and a Fisher Information Light indicator. Dynamic time warping and K-Means clustering were used to compare short-term urban activity and recovery trajectories.

Ongoing Comparative Research

Talent Mobility and Biomedical Innovation Systems in China and the United States

This project compares China and the United States through top biomedical scientists’ training, career mobility, research output, patent-related information, collaboration networks, and university-industry-research linkages. Current work centers on data organization, biography matching, network construction, and comparative analysis.

Earlier Research

Global Trade Network Evolution during COVID-19

This undergraduate research built directed weighted global trade networks from 2019-2021 UN Comtrade data covering 219 countries and regions. It compared centrality, core structure, product-type differences, and spatial organization before and during the COVID-19 period.
